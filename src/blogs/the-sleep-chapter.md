If you ask any parent about their newborn's sleep schedule, I can guarantee you'll get all sorts of answers filled with nostalgia, exhaustion, and joyful learnings. Because no matter how much you read up on it beforehand, nothing really prepares you for those newborn days when that tiny baby just has no interest in sleeping. Every parent has their own version of this story - and I'm sure most of them sound very familiar to each other.

For us, it took two full years before I could say with any confidence that he actually slept for a few hours in one go. Getting adjusted to the new routine was tough, but the baby's sleep was the hardest part of all. The early days were on and off every few hours - and even months later, a full night's sleep was still just a dream.

This is our story - not a guide, not a set of tips, just the honest account of how we found our rhythm in the middle of the night. If you're in the thick of it right now, half-asleep and wondering if this is normal - it is. You're not alone. And it won't last forever.

## The Beginning

When you're a first-time parent, you step into this whole new world with absolutely no prior experience. You take everything seriously - every sound, every twitch, every slightly-too-long nap. And you know what? There is nothing wrong with that. There's no such thing as being too careful when it comes to your newborn.

I remember we were asking so many questions to our son's pediatrician, and at one point we even told them that they might think we are being silly for asking every little thing. But honestly, I always believed that being curious and asking questions is so much better than just assuming things. Awareness is always better than shooting in the dark. We are responsible for a tiny human now, and there can't be any room for guesswork.

Our son fed constantly. I exclusively breastfed him, and those sessions would go on for hours - day and night, night and day. The routine was predictable in theory: feed, burp, fresh diaper, settle. And then he'd look up at us with those wide, bright eyes, completely unbothered by the concept of time, perfectly ready to be up for hours.

Apparently, those little power naps during feeding sessions were more than enough for him.

I always suspected babies have some kind of sixth sense - like they genuinely don't want their parents to sleep. They want their service every second of every day. That theory was confirmed every time we tried to transfer him to his crib. It didn't matter how deeply asleep he seemed, how slowly we moved, how carefully we lowered him down. The moment his back touched the mattress - eyes open. Wide open. As if he'd been awake the whole time.

## The Sleep Training Question

At some point, our pediatrician suggested sleep training. It's a well-researched approach, and for many families, it works beautifully. My husband and I sat down, talked it through seriously - and that was our one and only conversation about it.

I wasn't ready for it. And I want to be clear: that's not a judgment on anyone who chooses it. Parenting is deeply personal, and what works for one family might feel completely different for another. I didn't want to dismiss it without understanding it fully. I read everything - studies, personal essays, expert opinions. It only confirmed my decision.

My husband supported my call without hesitation. And I have never once second-guessed it. Sometimes you just have to trust what feels right for your own baby - and I've always believed in my instincts.

## How We Actually Made It Work

With sleep training off the table, we had to get creative. We didn't have a village. We didn't have any extra help - it was just the two of us, finding our way through it together.

The first thing we did was start learning his patterns. Every baby has a rhythm - it's not always the rhythm you want, but it's there. We started tracking when he was alert, when he was drowsy, when he fed most eagerly. Slowly, things started to make a little more sense.

We divided the nights. Not perfectly, not always fairly, but strategically. In the early months, I would take the first half since feeding was always my job anyway. My husband would take over somewhere around 3 or 4 AM - handling the diaper changes, the rocking, the settling - so I could get a few hours before morning. It wasn't a perfect system, and some nights it fell apart completely. But having that loose structure meant we weren't both running on empty at the same time.

We stopped expecting full nights of sleep and started celebrating two or three good hours in a row. The bar shifted - and surprisingly, so did our mood.

I was never a conventional "by the book" type of person. I leaned more towards what worked for our baby and our family. If a walk in the carrier gave us an hour of peace, we walked. If cluster feeding in the early evening led to a slightly longer stretch at night, we leaned into that too. We always prioritized our baby's cues and needs - sure, there are many forums for parenting tips and support, and while they are helpful, when it comes to our baby, the call is always ours.

Slowly, things shifted. Weeks turned to months. His stretches got longer. Things were finally starting to feel more like a routine and less like just getting through the night. By the time he turned two, I could finally say - okay, we are sleeping now. Not completely uninterrupted, but so much better than where we started.

## What I Want You to Take From This

I'm not sharing this because our story is remarkable or unique. It isn't. Many parents have lived these experiences, and many more will. I'm sharing it because when you're in the middle of it - running on fumes, questioning your decisions, doubting your choices - it helps to hear that this is just the season you're in, and it will change.

There is no single right way to get through the newborn stage. Sleep training, no sleep training. Co-sleeping, crib. Schedules, no schedules. What matters most is that you know your baby, trust yourself, and keep working together as parents. The fog does lift. The nights do get longer. And one day you'll look back at it and smile - or maybe even write about it. Trust me.

You're doing better than you think. Keep going.
