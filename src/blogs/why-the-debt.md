This might be a controversial, uncomfortable topic for many. But someone has to say it — so here I am, saying it.

In many cultures and belief systems, there exists this unspoken notion of burdening a child with the debt of simply being born. I never really understood it. Questioned it? Absolutely, many times. Did I ever get a clear answer? Never. Until one day, when I became a mother — and everything became clear.

## The Myth of the Unpayable Debt

Many of us grew up hearing — *"No one can repay the debt of a mother who nursed her child. It is an unpayable debt, one you carry for life, and can only return by being their support in old age."*

I always wondered why such a thought was projected in a mother's name. Who introduced it? Who glamorized it? And why do we keep passing it from generation to generation, unquestioned, like an heirloom nobody actually wants?

No one can burden a child with a debt for being born, for being fed, for being cared for — for simply existing in the life you chose to bring them into. A child never asked to be born. We needed them. The decision to bring a child into this world is ours — driven by our own wish for a complete family, our own longing to experience parenthood. So how can a child possibly carry a debt for something they never asked for?

## Parenting - My Take

The night my child was born, nobody handed me a ledger. There was no instruction manual either — just instinct, and love, and this tiny, scrunchy-faced little human I had been waiting months to meet — lying on my chest, breathing, real, mine.

And I just... knew. This is it. This is what I signed up for.

Not a debt. Not an obligation they'd someday have to repay. Just a choice I made — completely, willingly — and everything that came with it. The sleepless nights? Mine. The worry that never really goes away? Also mine. The joy that snuck up on me in the most ordinary moments? Mine too.

This was my longing, my choice, my blessing. All mine — and I wouldn't have it any other way.

As parents, we give our children values, love, cultural roots, and a sense of who they are. But after a certain point, they become the owners of their own decisions. Our role is to nudge them in the right direction, provide the best of what we have, and help them find their own path — but the final call is always theirs. That's not a failure of parenting. That's the whole point of it.

## Same Love, Different World

Children today are more socially aware, exposed to a wider range of ideas and experiences than generations before them. They are vocal, bold, and unafraid to question what doesn't make sense to them. If you ask me, that's not a problem. That's progress.

Parenting today means creating boundaries, communicating openly, building safe spaces, and actively choosing not to pass on what hurt us. What worked before doesn't work now — and honestly, a lot of what "worked before" was just silence and compliance dressed up as respect.

## What I Want You To Remember

We chose to become parents. Wholly, willingly, happily. That choice was ours — and everything that came with it was ours too. The moment we start expecting something back, we've stopped parenting and started keeping score.

So here's what I want my child to know.

You owe me nothing for being born. The moment I first held you, I don't even have the right words for what I felt. It was all of it at once — disbelief, overwhelm, something so real it almost didn't feel real. And somewhere in the middle of all that, something shifted in me. Something I didn't even know was there — until you were.

You owe me nothing for being loved. I chose you before I even held you — somewhere between the first kick and the first heartbeat. By the time you arrived, nothing else in the world mattered the same way anymore.

You owe me nothing for the life we built together — because it was never just yours or mine. It was ours. This family, this home, these memories we made in the most ordinary moments — the lazy Sunday mornings, the inside jokes, the routines that quietly became traditions. None of that was a sacrifice. That was just us, living. You didn't grow up in my care. We grew up together.

One day when you grow into someone who is happy, who is kind, who is honest, who chases a life they chose for themselves, who knows their own worth and guards it fiercely — I will consider myself the luckiest person alive. Because I got to witness every version of you before you became who you are today.

That was always, entirely, my privilege.
