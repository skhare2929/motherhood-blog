After our baby was born, whenever someone asked me about how the newborn phase was going, or how life was after becoming a mother - the first thing that used to come out of my mouth was: *"oh, it's tiring. It's a lot. I'm exhausted."* I don't remember that the immediate answer ever was - *"oh, we're doing great, we're enjoying this new life."* Those words did come, eventually - but only after I'd covered all the hard parts first.

Now I wonder, amidst all that settling into new life, learning to take care of a newborn, running on empty all the time - there were countless, priceless moments. Some of them are a blur, but a lot of them are clear as a picture. The coos, the lifting of the neck, the bobbly head, the newborn scrunches, the hand-on-face sleep. It was amazing. And we miss them a lot - sometimes even crave to relive those moments, more consciously, more present than we actually were at the time.

Sometimes when I go back a few years in my memory, I ask myself - how much of those early months do I actually remember? The answer, more often than not: not that much. But then I sit with it a little longer. I remember absorbing the moment of his first smile, but I don't remember the day or month when he first did it. I remember many moments when he just curled into me while feeding, the warmth of his breath, the way I'd look at him with this quiet, overwhelming wonder. Grateful just to be holding him that close.

So yeah - when I really think about it, I do remember a lot of those precious moments. It wasn't all exhaustion or burnout. There was joy being created right alongside all of it. It just doesn't get mentioned as often. It tends to get skipped over in the retelling of early parenting stories. But those moments are etched in us.

"Thank you for choosing us to be your parents." - those are the words I whispered to my son countless times, right from the moment I first held him. It wasn't something dramatic. It was exactly how I felt. How full my heart felt. Choosing to be a parent and getting to be a parent - those are very different things, in my opinion. Some people choose it and wait years for it to happen. Some people never get there. Some people get there without ever really choosing it. However you arrived at parenthood, there's something that shifts in you when you realize - this actually happened. This is real. And that shift, I think, is where the gratitude lives.

And maybe that's the honest truth of it - that you can be both exhausted and enchanted at the same time. That the tired and the tender live side by side, every single day. The early months weren't just hard. They were also full. And somewhere between the blur and the burnout, you were quietly collecting the best moments of your life.
